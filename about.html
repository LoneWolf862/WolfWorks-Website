About Wolf

I am a computer engineering student with a background in physics, currently pursuing adventures and exploits in applied robotics. My work primarily focuses on embedded systems and electronics engineering, spanning PCB design, control systems, instrumentation, firmware development, and full-system integration.

Most of the projects documented here are rooted in practical engineering: systems that can be built, tested, repaired, improved, and — most importantly — actually used. Nearly every project starts the same way: with curiosity, a problem that annoys me, or an idea that sounded significantly more reasonable at 1 AM than it did the following morning.

While electronics and embedded systems are usually at the center of my work, the overall scope has very few boundaries. Much like my ADHD, project creep tends to occur naturally and aggressively. One week I may be designing custom control electronics or diagnostic instrumentation; the next I am rebuilding servers, developing software tools, fabricating robotics hardware, doing automotive work, building lab equipment, landscaping, carpentry, networking infrastructure, or somehow all of the above simultaneously.

Outside of engineering, I grew up in Oregon and currently live in Idaho. I am an outdoorsman at heart and spend a large portion of my free time hiking, camping, shooting, climbing, skiing, and generally finding new ways to end up far from cell service. I am also an Eagle Scout, which probably explains at least part of my tendency to treat every bad idea as an expedition opportunity.

At the end of the day, I am still fundamentally just a child at heart with access to adult money, test equipment, power tools, and enough technical knowledge to occasionally question my own sanity.

Why No Face?

Even the not-so-observant among you may notice that absolutely nothing on this site traces back to my real identity. That is entirely intentional, and I have a couple of reasons for it.

I like sharing projects. I like building unique systems. I like documenting the engineering rabbit holes I voluntarily throw myself into at 2 AM. What I don’t like is the modern expectation that existing online requires turning your personal information into a searchable public database entry.

For one, I have done more than my fair share of internet sleuthing over the years, and it does not take much — a name, a face, a birthday, maybe an old username — before you can start reconstructing not only someone’s life story, but actual sensitive identity information. So you will have to forgive me for erring on the side of caution.

For two, many people across the country — and even around the world — already know me simply as “Wolf.” What started as a callsign and a gamertag slowly evolved into a nickname, and eventually became the placeholder for my identity whenever I operate online. At this point, even people in real life regularly refer to me as such. I see no need to change that.

So this site exists with intentional separation. No, the separation is not perfect, but the people who know me already know who I am, and the people who do not probably care far more about the work than my face attached to it.

For future employers, collaborators, or curious engineers wandering in from the depths of the internet: everything here is real, documented, and built by me. I simply prefer my personal information to flow in one direction with regard to this site and my online exploits.

That said, please do not hesitate to reach out if you have questions, want to collaborate, or simply wish to connect. You are more than welcome to contact me through email or through the form on my Discord server.

Wolf and AI

What is AI?

In order to describe my personal views on the use of AI, I first need to establish a basic and elementary understanding of how AI generally works, at least to my understanding at this time. Note that this explanation is an abstraction of complex principles and a conceptual simplification.

Modern LLMs are what I refer to as contextual vector models. This is a simplification; however, it retains the core principles of how they function. These AIs navigate a multi-dimensional vector model through plausibility, not truth.

Concepts and words, or tokens, are placed into a high-dimensional space where they have relative proximity to one another. Geometric proximity creates relationships between tokens, context creates relevance, and probability forms the basis of the model. Hallucinations are simply a kind of geometric drift.

A token can be thought of as a singular word or concept: a single point in this multi-dimensional vector space. A token by itself only has meaning through what it is near and through the context generated by its proximity to other ideas.

Because the model is not based on strict facts, it navigates regions of plausibility. It can therefore produce outputs that are logically and statistically coherent while still being completely void of factual basis.

At the end of the day, the model looks at what things tend to fit together, not necessarily what can or does exist. This is one common cause of hallucination.

How Wolf Uses AI

Let me start by saying that not all circumstances allow for the use of AI. There is a critical need for engineers and individuals in general to be able to function completely autonomously from the assistance of LLMs.

Large language models are incredible at translating, searching for relevant information, and adapting that information to current work. However, they inevitably lack the fundamental direction and contextual understanding that you possess for your own project.

For these reasons, I believe AI should never be used in the workplace to reason, synthesize, or conclude. These are critical steps that the human element must perform.

I would not give a large language model a general purpose and simply tell it to write code that fulfills that purpose. I would, however, ask it for examples of how to perform a certain function, or for advice comparing different implementation methods.

Another way of putting this is that I would write the pseudocode myself, potentially with input from an LLM to explore more efficient possibilities. I would then allow the LLM to expand that pseudocode into the proper syntax of a specific programming language.

LLMs are inherently excellent at formatting and presentation. The problems arise when people attempt to use them to generate original reasoning or content rather than refine content that already exists.

In lab reports, an AI should not touch or conclude anything relating to the data, interpretation, or procedure of an experiment. It can improve grammar, spelling, formatting, and clarity, but it should not independently correct, reinterpret, or alter experimental conclusions.

In fact, this entire section was written with the assistance of an LLM using exactly this principle. I provided the concepts and ideas, and the LLM helped reorganize them into clearer professional English under my supervision.

AI is not a tool for things you cannot do. If you can already do something and understand it, but it will take a long time, then that is a proper use case for AI.
