Project

P.A.C.E

Industrial Automation, Controls Engineering, Instrumentation, and PLC Systems

Active Development — Hardware Procurement and Assembly Phase

Short Description

P.A.C.E. is a compact portable industrial controls environment designed for PLC programming, automation experiments, instrumentation, and control-system education. It is built to provide a realistic control-panel experience outside of a dedicated industrial lab.

Project Overview

P.A.C.E. stands for Portable Automation and Control Environment. The project is a compact, bench-deployable control panel intended to function as a universal automation platform for PLC work, instrumentation, industrial logic, and hardware integration.

The goal is to build a realistic industrial-style control environment that can interface with many different external projects without requiring a full permanent installation. Rather than being a single-purpose machine, P.A.C.E. is designed as a reusable platform for controls testing, education, troubleshooting, and prototyping.

The system combines 120 VAC input power, 24 VDC control power, industrial operator controls, analog and digital I/O, DB-25 field connectors, DIN rail mounted components, terminal blocks, meters, indicators, and safety devices into one portable package.

Features

  • Portable industrial training environment designed to provide real-world control panel experience outside of a manufacturing facility.
  • Compact steel enclosure with industrial front-panel controls and instrumentation.
  • 120 VAC input power through a fused locking inlet system.
  • Integrated 24 VDC supply for control power and field devices.
  • Emergency stop, master power switch, pushbuttons, HOA switches, toggle switches, key switch, and indicator lights.
  • Analog voltage meters for live system monitoring and visual feedback.
  • DB-25 based field I/O interface for rapid external connection to projects and test hardware.
  • Separate analog and digital input/output organization for cleaner PLC interfacing.
  • DIN rail terminal block architecture for maintainability, serviceability, and future expansion.
  • Designed around industrial wiring conventions, ferrules, structured routing, and color-coded conductors.
  • Intended to support Allen-Bradley PLC hardware, analog cards, external sensors, actuators, and lab automation projects.

Technical Challenges

  • Compact panel layout: fitting industrial controls, meters, terminal blocks, power distribution, relays, and connectors into a small portable enclosure while maintaining serviceability.
  • Universal I/O design: creating a connector and terminal architecture flexible enough to support many future projects without needing major rewiring.
  • Power separation: managing 120 VAC input power, 24 VDC control power, signal wiring, analog wiring, and equipment grounding in a safe and understandable layout.
  • Industrial realism vs. budget: selecting components that feel and behave like real industrial panel hardware while keeping the project practical and affordable.
  • Operator-interface density: placing enough switches, lights, meters, and control devices on the front panel to be useful without making the interface cramped or confusing.
  • Wiring standards: developing a color-coded wiring scheme that follows industrial convention while remaining easy to understand during instruction and troubleshooting.
  • Future expansion: designing the panel so additional PLCs, test fixtures, sensors, relays, indicators, and external systems can be added later without rebuilding the core architecture.

Miscellaneous Notes

P.A.C.E. is intended to act as a portable controls lab rather than a finished appliance. The value of the system is in its flexibility: it can be used to test PLC logic, simulate field devices, interface with external machines, teach wiring practices, and provide a reusable hardware environment for future automation projects.

The project also serves as a practical exercise in industrial panel design, including enclosure layout, wire routing, DIN rail organization, terminal planning, device labeling, I/O planning, and safe power distribution.
