Project

Pneumatic Briefcase

Robotics Infrastructure, Pneumatics, and Field Diagnostics

Completed / Operational Deployment

Short Description

A portable pneumatic support system designed for FRC robotics troubleshooting, leak isolation, prototyping, and subsystem testing. The unit contained a complete pneumatic backbone in a field-deployable briefcase that could temporarily replace portions of a robot's pneumatic system during diagnostics.

Project Overview

The Pneumatic Briefcase was developed as a companion project to Brain Box. While Brain Box provided a known-good electrical and control backbone, the Pneumatic Briefcase provided a known-good pneumatic backbone.

During robot testing and competition, diagnosing pneumatic issues could be difficult because failures might originate from compressors, tanks, manifolds, regulators, valves, tubing, or the mechanism itself. Isolating these problems often required powering the entire robot, which slowed down troubleshooting and introduced additional variables.

To solve this problem, I built a complete pneumatic system into a portable briefcase. The unit contained a compressor, reserve air storage, manifold, fittings, and supporting pneumatic hardware. This allowed the briefcase to temporarily replace portions of a robot's pneumatic system for testing and diagnosis.

By connecting the Pneumatic Briefcase directly to robot mechanisms, we could quickly determine whether a problem originated in the robot's pneumatic infrastructure or elsewhere in the system. The unit also proved useful for prototyping, subsystem testing, and leak detection.

Features

  • Portable field-deployable pneumatic backbone.
  • Integrated compressor system.
  • Reserve air storage tanks.
  • Pneumatic manifold and distribution hardware.
  • Designed for rapid robot troubleshooting.
  • Known-good pneumatic reference system.
  • Leak isolation and diagnostic capability.
  • Subsystem testing without powering the full robot.
  • Useful for mechanism prototyping and development.
  • Portable briefcase enclosure for transport and deployment.
  • Companion tool to the Brain Box diagnostic platform.

Technical Challenges

  • Packaging a complete pneumatic system into a portable enclosure.
  • Creating a known-good pneumatic reference that could be trusted during troubleshooting.
  • Supporting rapid deployment under competition and testing conditions.
  • Allowing robot mechanisms to be tested independently of the robot's installed pneumatic infrastructure.
  • Maintaining portability while retaining useful air capacity and flow.
  • Designing a system robust enough for transportation and field use.

Miscellaneous Notes

Like Brain Box, the Pneumatic Briefcase was not designed to improve robot performance directly. Instead, it was intended to improve the team's ability to diagnose, repair, prototype, and operate pneumatic systems efficiently.

The project originated from a simple philosophy: troubleshooting is much easier when you have a complete system that you know works correctly. By temporarily replacing parts of the robot's pneumatic infrastructure with a known-good system, failures could be isolated quickly and with far less guesswork.

Although significantly simpler than many robot subsystems, the Pneumatic Briefcase proved to be a valuable support tool and became part of a broader effort to create dedicated diagnostic infrastructure for the team.
